“It’s like a jungle sometimes, it makes me wonder how I keep from goin’ under.” These are the opening lines to “The Message” by Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five. Like Upton Sinclair and his book The Jungle (released 76 years before “The Message”), Grandmaster Flash wanted to shed light on the circumstances of the time. Both wanted to elevate the pain and suffering of exploited people into the minds and hearts of the country. Both wanted to see a change. Both wanted - needed - to speak a message. That is the power of hip-hop culture and rap. The ability to speak to one’s circumstances in a way that transcends those same circumstances. Political and social messaging was integral to the development of hip-hop, and continues to influence artists working in the genre today.

## Part I: The Groundwork
In the early days of hip-hop, the culture was focused on the party scene. Even so, the social need for hip-hop was nascent and growing. Hip-hop was a genre created by Blacks and Latinos in the early 1970s, two groups that society had largely written off. They were marginalized, pushed off to the side, living in horrible conditions. The parties of DJ Kool Herc were an escape from that, an escape from the gang warfare and the hunger and the racism. A place where one could go and have fun, without worrying about the thousands of pounds of headaches dragged around all day. So although this very early era wasn’t known for “speaking truth to power”, it was culturally and politically charged even if the people participating in creating this community weren’t aware of it.

One person who was aware of how the culture of hip-hop could bring people together was Afrika Bambaataa. Bambaataa, born Lance Taylor, started out as a member of the Black Spades, a street gang in New York City. After attending some early hip-hop parties, he saw how it could unify. Bambaataa decided to dedicate his life towards “peace, love, unity, and having fun”, what he describes as the values of hip-hop. He started throwing his own hip-hop parties, with the goal of bringing together rival gang members, to help them figure out their differences and build the “Universal Zulu Nation”. The Universal Zulu Nation was an alternative to these street gangs, that gave young kids in the tri-state area a purpose so they didn’t have to resort to hustling or other street activities. Once again, although the hip-hop music of the time did not explicitly contain many social or political messages, the culture growing around it - and with it - did. 

Hip-hop is not just the music, either. It encompasses a whole culture and way of living. From the b-boys and b-girls dancing the night away, to the graffiti artists tagging the highest locations they can get to, every aspect of hip-hop culture was a means of escapism for the people pushed to the sidelines in America. Graffiti is probably the most contested aspect of hip-hop; many people wonder if it truly is a part of hip-hop culture. Grandmaster Flash, one of the early pioneers in DJing, has said "You know what bugs me, they put hip-hop with graffiti. How do they intertwine?". From its own contemporary beginnings (as “graffiti” in many forms can be found in Ancient Greece and the like), graffiti was a way to stand up to the power and leave a message for all to see. Even the act itself is defiant; putting aerosol paint on a building you do not own or have permission to change, you are breaking the law. Even if graffiti wasn’t directly influenced by hip-hop, it contains many of the same cultural and social ideals that permeate throughout hip-hop culture.

Outside of hip-hop culture, political and social messaging was on the rise in music, especially with African American artists. The Black Power movement that started in the mid 1960s had a plethora of music to help back it up (as the best socio-political movements do), and “Black music, prayers, and religious and religious doctrines were refashioned to critique Black oppression” (Morris, 1999). Marvin Gaye (“Inner City Blues”), James Brown (“Say It Loud”), and Billy Paul (“Am I Black Enough for You?”) were just some of the artists who were taking the movement into their own hands, giving the people fighting for their rights a rallying cry, and showing those in the movement and those in opposition what they were fighting for.

This led to an atmosphere ripe for politically and socially conscious rap. Although partying was always fun (and sometimes a necessary distraction), there were too many issues plaguing those early members of the hip-hop community. With their brothers and sisters in other musical genres standing up to power, and the peace and love messages of Afrika Bambaataa, it was only a matter of time until someone had something serious to say in the hip-hop community.

## Part II: The Early Consciousness

By 1982, the hip-hop community was ready to move on from the party scene which created it. Although the same musical trappings were still there, such as disco beats over which an MC spit some bars, the genre was ripe for some more elevated lyricism. The first successful song to have a social message was “The Message” by Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five. While this song is important for a multitude of reasons, it wasn’t the first socially conscious rap song. The consensus is that credit goes to “How We Gonna Make the Black Nation Rise?” by Brother D featuring Collective Effort. Originally released in 1980, Brother D (born Daryl Aamaa Nubyahn) was a school teacher who “noticed kids around [his] block doing rap, but there was no message.” He decided to write a rap song that wasn’t just braggadocio and partying, but actually had something meaningful to say, especially for his students. The song even took aim at those partiers, as can be heard in the first verse:

> While you party down yellin', "Shock the house  
> Get down, rock shock the house!"  
> The Ku Klux Klan is on the loose  
> Training their kids in machine gun use  

“How We Gonna Make the Black Nation Rise?” is full of lyrics like this that had a deeper meaning (another being “America was built, understand/By stolen labor on stolen land”), that showed the party animals who founded hip-hop that they still needed to be vigilant and conscious to the terror that could be lurking just a few blocks away. This song, however, didn’t garner much attention past Brother D’s students. 

“The Message”, on the other hand, amassed an incredible amount of praise for a rap song at the time. It regularly places in the “Top Songs of All Time” lists and its effects on the genre can still be felt today. Prior to this song, when people thought “hip-hop” they immediately thought of DJs spinning records, b-boys breaking, and just the general atmosphere of a party. “The Message,” however, was able to deconstruct the genre up to this point and speak to the minds of people listening to the song. It outlined the horrible conditions people in the South Bronx were living in. The opening lines (“It’s like a jungle sometimes, it makes me wonder how I keep from goin’ under”) shed light on the mental state of the people living there, and then continued to paint a vivid picture of what the area was truly like:

> Broken glass everywhere  
> People pissing on the stairs, you know they just don't care  
> I can't take the smell, can't take the noise  
> Got no money to move out, I guess I got no choice  
> Rats in the front room, roaches in the back  
> Junkies in the alley with a baseball bat  
> I tried to get away but I couldn't get far  
> 'Cause a man with a tow truck repossessed my car  

Melle Mel and Duke Bootee (the two main vocalists on this track) were able to bring the streets of the South Bronx to their listeners. The song also helped cement the MC as the main focal point of hip-hop, as opposed to the spinning and scratching DJs who had up until that point usually taken center stage.

“The Message” laid the groundwork for what people today call “conscious rap”. Conscious rap can be defined as having “black nationalist sound, image, and message” (Decker, 1993), while also encouraging “critique of social and political conditions” (Mohammed-Akinyela, 2012). While “The Message” wasn’t an overtly political song, it did have a strong message for those listening, and went against the grain of hip-hop music at the time. The track opened up the door for other socially conscious rappers to come to the forefront.

## Part III: The Explosion

With the huge success of “The Message”, other artists saw that they could use this new genre for a wide array of artistic expression. Artists like Public Enemy and Ice-T (who at the time were considered “hardcore” rappers) regularly talked about the issues plaguing their local communities and the African American community at large. On his first major hit, “6 in the Mornin’”, Ice-T rapped about Los Angeles gang life in a way that not many had before. In the opening hook, Ice-T raps:

> 6 ’n the morning’ police at my door  
> Fresh Adidas squeak across the bathroom floor  
> Out the back window I make a escape  
> Don’t even have a chance to grab my old school tape  

In the song, Ice-T depicts “an adversarial relationship with police in which people are presumed to be guilty of crimes, often because of race or suspected gang association” (Hollander, 2014). 

At the other end of the country, in Long Island, Public Enemy had started to solidify what a conscious rapper was, and helped to usher in the incoming wave of hardcore/gangsta rap. P.E. was “rock ‘n’ roll simply played a lot faster”, according to frontman Chuck D, with all of the social commentary that tended to come with rock. The group had defined a new sound with more “street” arrangements, more aggressive beats, and much more political lyrics. Their music called on the listener to question their surroundings and come up with their own view of what their world could be. It wasn’t just the lyrics that were bringing in their fans, the complex musical structure and polyrhythms necessitated multiple listenings to each track to truly take in everything it had to offer. They were masters of the sample, taking tracks, beats, and sounds that normally wouldn’t be found in a hardcore hip-hop track and just making it work. Public Enemy was a force to be reckoned with in their heyday, and they used their significant power to help bridge their message into the white mainstream. Up to this point rap and hip-hop were seen by many as a wholly African American, Latino, and minority community. Public Enemy decided to help bring in white listeners to their movement, by collaborating with artists from other genres like heavy-metal and punk rock. This proved not only helpful in spreading their message, but also increased their commercial success.

Not every artist in the late 80s and early 90s was as wholly political as Public Enemy. Groups like N.W.A. were starting to take over the gangsta rap subgenre, and weren’t considered as much of a movement as Public Enemy. However, that doesn’t mean they still didn’t have a message for their listeners. In songs like “F--- Tha Police” N.W.A. told their listeners to stand up to the police brutality that plagued the streets of their hometown Compton, a message that resonated with young people across the country.

This led to artists like Tupac Shakur who redefined socially conscious rap, and whose life, ironically, was cut short by the violence that he eschewed by focusing on the social aspects in his music.

These pioneers in using rap and hip-hop as a means of political and social expression were integral to the development of the genre as a political and social force. They forever changed the fabric of what hip-hop stood for, and those effects can still be felt to this day.


## Part IV: The Modern Day
In the 21st century, there is such an incredibly wide array of artists that fall under the hip-hop umbrella. There is no shortage of socially conscious rappers for listeners to choose from. Artists like Lupe Fiasco are continuing in the footsteps of Public Enemy by not only rapping about the issues that are important to them, but remaining commercially viable and popular throughout their career. On the song “Strange Fruition”, Fiasco raps:

> Now I can't pledge allegiance to your flag  
> Cause I can't find no reconciliation with your past  
> When there was nothing equal for my people in your math  
> You forced us in the ghetto and then you took our dads  

Fiasco expertly attacks the unfettered nationalism inherent in the United States, while making references to African American history and subjugation of them, such as the three-fifths compromise (“there was nothing equal for my people in your math”). The title of the song even references “Strange Fruit”, a seminal song that helped to usher in the Civil Rights movement and is known as one of the first protest songs. While “Strange Fruit” attacked the overt racism of the day (with lynchings being an all too common occurrence), Fiasco discusses the institutional and hidden racism that permeates the fabric of the country.

In the modern age, hip-hop has also spread around the world, leaving the door open for rappers and artists from other countries to speak their voice. slowthai is one of the quintessential examples of a modern conscious rapper. In his songs he discusses issues like Brexit and his dislike of Theresa May (the former prime minister who oversaw much of the Brexit discussions). One of his most popular (and contentious) songs is “Nothing Great About Britain”, attacking the British “powers that be” the same way as “F--- Tha Police” and “The Message” attacked the American.

A very interesting development in modern hip-hop has been bringing the genre to the Broadway stage, none as apparent as Hamilton in 2016. The acceptance of this rap musical, centered around a revered “founding father” of America shows the level that rap and hip-hop have been integrated into American society. The musical, written by Lin-Manuel Miranda, offers a look into the founding of the United States through the eyes of Alexander Hamilton, told with rap and r&b (with some generic musical theatre thrown in). The second act of the show deals with the creation of the political systems we have today after the end of the Revolutionary War. The whole show is littered with references to conscious rap. One that really stands out is in the song “Cabinet Battle #1”. The character of Thomas Jefferson raps “Sometimes it makes me wonder why I even bring the thunder”. The lyrics, beat, and intonation of this line show a clear reference to “The Message”, a true test of the song’s permanence as a fixture of political rap.

## Part V: The Conclusion

From its roots, hip-hop has had consciousness in its DNA. Afrika Bambaataa wanted to use its power to bring together rival gangs and end the violence. Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five wanted to show the world the conditions their brothers and sisters were forced to live in. N.W.A. wanted to stand up to the police brutality that was inherent in living in a city as a minority. These artists spearheaded what we now know today as conscious rap, and artists like slowthai and even Broadway musicals continue to be influenced by their “Message”.
